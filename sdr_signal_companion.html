Chapter 0 · The math beneath

The circle behind every sine, the bell curve behind every noise floor

Where sin actually comes from · what A, f and φ really are · why noise is "Gaussian" and what σ means

Why start here

Every chapter ahead leans on two mathematical objects. The first is the sine wave $A\sin(2\pi f t+\phi)$ — every carrier, subcarrier and tone in this companion is one of these, or a sum of them. The second is the Gaussian distribution — every time a lab says "add noise σ" or a spec says "Gaussian-smoothed", that bell curve is doing the work. Neither needs to be taken on faith, and both fit in one short chapter.

Sine is a circle, unrolled

$\sin$ is not an arbitrary squiggle that physics happens to like. Put a point on a circle of radius 1 and ask: how high is it? That height, as a function of the angle you have turned through, is the sine function. Spin the point at a steady rate and write its height down over time — the wave draws itself. Every symbol in $A\sin(2\pi ft+\phi)$ is a property of that spinning point.

Randomness has a shape

Noise is random, but random is not lawless. Add up millions of tiny independent disturbances — the thermal jostling of electrons in your antenna and amplifier — and the total follows one specific, predictable shape: the bell curve. A single number, the standard deviation σ, tells you how wide that bell is, and therefore how loud the noise floor is.

Where it shows up

Chapter 1 turns the spinning circle into NumPy arrays. Chapter 4 promotes it to a point spinning in the complex plane. Chapter 9's GFSK uses the bell curve not as noise but as a smoothing filter. And every SNR readout in every lab below is a ratio between a sine's power and a Gaussian's σ². This chapter is the dictionary for all of that.

0.1 The sine function — the height of a spinning point

Trigonometry starts with a right triangle: for an angle θ, $\sin\theta$ is defined as the ratio of the side opposite the angle to the hypotenuse, and $\cos\theta$ as the adjacent side to the hypotenuse — the school mnemonic SOH-CAH-TOA. The quiet miracle, and the reason these ratios deserve names at all, is that they depend only on the angle, never on the triangle's size: scale the triangle up and every side grows in lockstep, so each ratio is frozen. One angle therefore maps to one fixed value of $\sin$ and $\cos$ — and, read backwards, one pair of sides maps back to one angle. (That backwards reading is exactly what np.angle does in Chapter 4 when it recovers a phase from an (I, Q) pair.)

On a circle of radius 1, the triangle picture collapses into something simpler. Draw a radius at angle θ from the positive x-axis: the radius is the hypotenuse, and its length is 1, so the ratios become the sides themselves. The point on the rim sits at exactly $(\cos\theta,\ \sin\theta)$. Cosine is the point's horizontal position; sine is its height. Nothing more — and unlike the triangle, the circle keeps working past 90°, for any angle at all.

Now sweep θ from 0 all the way around and watch only the height: it starts at 0, rises to 1 at a quarter-turn, falls back through 0 at a half-turn, dips to −1 at three quarters, and returns. Plot that height against the angle and you have drawn the sine wave. The wave repeats because the circle closes: after one full turn the point is back where it started, so $\sin(\theta + 2\pi) = \sin\theta$, forever. Periodicity isn't an extra property of sine — it is the circle. Cosine, the horizontal shadow, traces the identical wave a quarter-turn ahead: $\cos\theta = \sin(\theta + \pi/2)$. Two copies of one wave, 90° apart — keep that image; it becomes the I/Q pair at the heart of Chapter 4.

∴ Building $A\sin(2\pi f t + \phi)$ one symbol at a time

Chapter 1 calls this "the simplest signal". Here is where every piece of it comes from.

  1. Start with the height: $\sin\theta$ is the vertical coordinate of a point at angle θ on the unit circle. So far the input is an angle, not time.
  2. Make it spin: a radio tone repeats $f$ times per second, so the angle must grow by $f$ full turns — $f\times 2\pi$ radians — every second: $\theta(t) = 2\pi f t$. Frequency is nothing but rotation rate. (The product $2\pi f$, in radians per second, is often written ω and called angular frequency.)
  3. Choose where it starts: at $t=0$ the point doesn't have to sit at angle zero. Its starting angle φ is the phase offset — a head start or delay on the spin: $\theta(t) = 2\pi f t + \phi$. Two tones with identical frequency can differ only in φ — which is exactly the property phase-shift keying (Chapter 7) modulates.
  4. Choose the size of the circle: a radius of $A$ instead of 1 scales the height everywhere: $x(t) = A\sin(2\pi f t + \phi)$. $A$ is the amplitude — the knob AM and OOK (Chapter 7) turn.
Takeaway: $A\sin(2\pi ft+\phi)$ is not a formula to memorize, it is a sentence — "the height of a point on a circle of radius $A$, spinning $f$ times per second, that started at angle φ." Amplitude, frequency, phase: the three knobs every modulation scheme in this document turns.

Radians, and why 2π is everywhere

A radian measures an angle by arc length on a circle of radius 1: walk a distance of one radius along the rim and you have swept 1 radian. The full rim is $2\pi r$ long, so a full turn is $2\pi$ radians — which is why "$f$ cycles per second" becomes "$2\pi f$ radians per second" inside every sine: 2π radians per cycle, times $f$ cycles per second. That is the only job the $2\pi$ does in every equation in this document. Chapter 4 returns to radians when the spinning point moves to the complex plane.

0.2 The Gaussian — what "add noise σ" actually does

A distribution answers the question: if I draw a random value, which values do I get and how often? Draw thousands of values and pile them into a histogram and the distribution is the shape the pile takes. The Gaussian (or "normal") distribution is the most important shape in all of measurement: a symmetric bell centred on a mean μ (zero, for noise), falling off smoothly on both sides. Its width is set by one number, the standard deviation σ — the typical distance of a sample from the centre. The bell's equation is $p(v)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-v^2/2\sigma^2}$, but you will never need to evaluate it by hand; what matters is the shape and the σ.

σ comes with a remarkably useful rule of thumb: about 68% of samples land within ±1σ of the mean, 95% within ±2σ, and 99.7% within ±3σ — whatever σ is. So "noise with σ = 0.3" means: most samples nudge the signal by less than ±0.3, a few by up to ±0.6, and almost none by more than ±0.9. One number describes the entire noise floor.

∴ Why noise is Gaussian — "AWGN", unpacked word by word

Receiver noise isn't Gaussian by convention. It's Gaussian by theorem.

  1. The noise at your antenna is a sum. The voltage wobble in any conductor above absolute zero is the combined effect of billions of electrons being thermally jostled, each contributing a minuscule independent push (Johnson–Nyquist noise — the same Nyquist as Chapter 2).
  2. The central limit theorem: add up many small independent random contributions and the total follows the bell curve — regardless of the distribution of each individual piece. This is why the Gaussian appears everywhere from RF to test scores: it is the universal shape of accumulated randomness.
  3. "Additive": the noise simply adds to the signal, sample by sample: $x_{rx}[n] = x[n] + \text{noise}[n]$. It doesn't multiply it or distort it.
  4. "White": each noise sample is independent of the last, so no frequency is favoured — the noise spreads its power evenly across the whole spectrum, the way white light contains every colour. On an FFT plot it is the flat "floor" the real spikes must rise above.
Conclusion: AWGN — additive white Gaussian noise — is the standard model of a real receiver, and x + np.random.normal(0, sigma, n) is its entire simulation. Every noise slider in this companion runs exactly that line.

Gaussian noise in one line — and the SNR formula behind every lab readout:

import numpy as np

sigma = 0.3
noise = np.random.normal(0, sigma, 4096)  # mean 0, std-dev sigma: the bell curve, sampled

x_rx = x + noise                # "add Gaussian noise" is literally this line

np.mean(np.abs(noise) < sigma)      # -> ~0.68   68% of samples within ±1 sigma
np.mean(np.abs(noise) < 2*sigma)    # -> ~0.95   95% within ±2 sigma

# noise power = sigma**2 (the variance); a sine's power = A**2 / 2
snr_db = 10*np.log10((A**2/2) / sigma**2)   # the "SNR (dB)" readout in every lab

Decibels — the unit every SNR readout uses

Power ratios in radio span absurd ranges — a received signal can carry a millionth of a millionth of the transmitted power — so RF speaks in logarithms. A power ratio $P_1/P_0$ expressed in decibels is $10\log_{10}(P_1/P_0)$ dB. Three values are worth memorizing: +3 dB ≈ ×2 in power, +10 dB = ×10, +20 dB = ×100; negative dB are the same factors in reverse, and 0 dB means "equal". Logarithms also turn multiplication into addition, so a chain of gains and losses simply sums. SNR — signal-to-noise ratio — is a power ratio, hence dB: a readout of "10 dB" means the signal carries ten times the noise power; "−3 dB" means the noise has twice the power of the signal.

Practice problems

1. A lab generates a tone of amplitude $A=1.0$ and adds AWGN with standard deviation $\sigma=0.25$. Using a sine's power $A^2/2$, noise power $\sigma^2$, and $\text{SNR}_\text{dB}=10\log_{10}\!\big(\tfrac{A^2/2}{\sigma^2}\big)$, what SNR does the readout show?

Show solution
  1. Signal power. $A^2/2 = 1.0^2/2 = 0.5$.
  2. Noise power. $\sigma^2 = 0.25^2 = 0.0625$.
  3. Take the ratio in dB. $10\log_{10}(0.5/0.0625) = 10\log_{10}(8)$.
Answer: $\approx 9.03$ dB — the tone carries about 8× the noise power, a comfortably readable signal.

2. A receiver's noise floor is modelled as Gaussian with $\sigma=0.4$. Using the rule that about $99.7\%$ of samples fall within $\pm3\sigma$ of the mean, what is the largest noise nudge you should expect on essentially every sample?

Show solution
  1. Pick the band. "Essentially every sample" means the $\pm3\sigma$ window (99.7%).
  2. Scale by σ. $3\sigma = 3 \times 0.4 = 1.2$.
Answer: $\pm1.2$ — almost no sample strays beyond this, so a clean signal must rise well above 1.2 to survive.

3. An amplifier raises a signal's voltage by a factor of $5$. Voltage gain in decibels is $20\log_{10}(V_1/V_0)$. What is the gain in dB?

Show solution
  1. Use the voltage form. Voltage (an amplitude) uses the $20\log_{10}$ rule, not $10\log_{10}$.
  2. Plug in the ratio. $20\log_{10}(5)$.
Answer: $\approx 14.0$ dB — a 5× voltage boost is a 25× power boost, and $10\log_{10}(25)$ gives the same 14 dB.
Chapter 1 · The signal is an array

Before you receive a signal, learn to build one

NumPy · the four numbers behind every waveform: sample rate, frequency, amplitude, phase

What an SDR actually gives you

A software-defined radio does one thing: it measures the voltage at its antenna many millions of times a second and hands you the numbers. There is no "station", no "audio" — just a long array of samples. To understand what comes in, it pays to first generate signals you already understand and inspect them. Everything in this companion is numpy arrays and a plot.

The idea

A pure tone is the simplest signal: $x(t)=A\sin(2\pi f t+\phi)$ — the height of a spinning point, built symbol by symbol in Chapter 0. In code, time is not continuous — it is a grid of instants $t_n = n/f_s$, where the sample rate $f_s$ is how many samples per second you take. Pick $f_s$, build the time vector, and the whole waveform is one line of NumPy.

The test

Add two tones and you get a beat pattern; add Gaussian noise and you get something that looks like a real capture. Real receivers see a sum of every signal in range plus noise — and yet, as later chapters show, the Fourier transform pulls each one back out. Building that sum yourself first makes the decode obvious.

What it founds

Every script in this folder begins exactly here. nfc_modulation_demo.py synthesizes an entire ISO 14443 waveform from np.sin and a gate array before it ever touches a capture — so you can see each layer of abstraction in isolation. Synthesis is the debugger of DSP.

A single tone, the way every lab below starts — and the way nfc_modulation_demo.py builds its 847.5 kHz subcarrier:

import numpy as np

fs = 1_000_000          # sample rate: 1,000,000 samples per second (1 MS/s)
f  = 50_000             # tone frequency: 50 kHz
A  = 1.0                # amplitude
phi = 0.0               # starting phase (radians)

n = 4096                       # number of samples to generate
t = np.arange(n) / fs          # time vector in seconds: [0, 1/fs, 2/fs, ...]
x = A * np.sin(2*np.pi*f*t + phi)

# x is now a real-valued array. That is the entire signal.
# samples per cycle = fs / f = 20  -> 20 dots trace out each sine period

∴ Why the time vector is np.arange(n)/fs

Three lines turn "a frequency in Hz" into "an array of voltages".

  1. A real sensor samples at fixed instants spaced $\Delta t = 1/f_s$ apart. np.arange(n) is $[0,1,2,\dots]$ sample indices; dividing by $f_s$ turns them into seconds.
  2. The argument of the sine must accumulate $2\pi$ radians of phase per cycle, $f$ cycles per second: hence $2\pi f t$. One full cycle every $1/f$ seconds.
  3. Sampling means evaluating the continuous $\sin$ only on that grid. The array is the signal as far as the computer — and the radio — is concerned. Nothing exists between the samples.
Takeaway: change $f_s$ and you change how finely time is diced; change $f$ and you change how fast phase spins. Those two numbers, and their ratio, govern everything from here on.

The noise slider and the SNR readout, precisely

"Noise σ" adds an independent draw from Chapter 0's bell curve to every sample: x + np.random.normal(0, sigma, n). σ is the typical size of each bump, and σ² is the noise power. The tone's power is $A^2/2$ — the average of $\sin^2$ over a cycle is exactly ½ — so the SNR stat above is $10\log_{10}\!\big(\tfrac{A^2/2}{\sigma^2}\big)$ in decibels (both defined in Chapter 0). Crank σ up and watch the dB fall: every "weak signal" in the rest of this companion is exactly this picture.

Practice problems

1. You generate a $125$ kHz tone captured at $f_s = 2$ MS/s. The "samples per cycle" readout is $f_s/f$. How many samples trace out each period of the sine?

Show solution
  1. Form the ratio. samples/cycle $= f_s / f = 2{,}000{,}000 / 125{,}000$.
  2. Divide. $= 16$.
Answer: $16$ samples per cycle — far above the Nyquist floor of 2, so the sine looks smooth rather than jagged.

2. A synthetic tone has amplitude $A=0.5$ and the noise slider is set to $\sigma=0.1$. With signal power $A^2/2$ and noise power $\sigma^2$, what SNR (in dB) does the lab report?

Show solution
  1. Signal power. $A^2/2 = 0.25/2 = 0.125$.
  2. Noise power. $\sigma^2 = 0.01$.
  3. dB ratio. $10\log_{10}(0.125/0.01) = 10\log_{10}(12.5)$.
Answer: $\approx 11.0$ dB — even at half amplitude the tone sits well above this light noise.

3. You build a time vector with $t = $ np.arange(n)/fs at $f_s = 1$ MS/s and want exactly $10$ ms of signal. How many samples $n$ must you generate?

Show solution
  1. Duration relation. The capture spans $n/f_s$ seconds, so $n = \text{duration} \times f_s$.
  2. Plug in. $n = 0.010 \text{ s} \times 1{,}000{,}000 \text{ /s}$.
Answer: $n = 10{,}000$ samples — each millisecond of air time costs 1000 samples at this rate.
Chapter 2 · The one rule you cannot break

Sampling and the Nyquist limit

Why a 600 kHz tone can masquerade as a 400 kHz tone — and how the decoders pick their sample rate

What was known

Sampling throws away everything between the sample instants. The astonishing result — the Nyquist–Shannon theorem — is that nothing is lost provided the signal contains no frequency at or above half the sample rate. That half-rate, $f_s/2$, is the Nyquist limit. Below it, the samples determine the waveform uniquely; above it, they lie.

The idea

A tone above Nyquist does not vanish — it aliases, folding back to a lower frequency that fits the samples equally well. A 600 kHz tone sampled at 1 MS/s is indistinguishable from a 400 kHz tone: every sample agrees. The radio cannot tell them apart, so an analog anti-alias filter must remove the out-of-band energy before the ADC.

The test

The folding formula is exact: an input at $f$ appears at $f_\text{alias}=\lvert f - f_s\,\text{round}(f/f_s)\rvert$. Push $f$ past $f_s/2$ in the lab and watch the reconstructed tone slide back down. This is also how a cheap RTL-SDR can "see" a signal you tuned past — and why undersampling on purpose (bandpass sampling) is a real technique.

What it founds

Every decoder here picks a sample rate well above its symbol rate. decode_fsk.py captures at 2 MS/s for a 50 ksym/s link — 40 samples/symbol. That oversampling is what lets it average each symbol (a boxcar matched filter) and slice cleanly under noise.

2.1 Sampling is taking snapshots — and snapshots can lie

A movie is nothing but a row of still photographs shown in quick succession. Shoot enough frames per second — film runs at 24, video at 30 or 60 — and the eye stitches them back into continuous motion. Shoot too few and the moments between the frames are simply gone: a fast-moving ball is photographed only here and there, so on screen it appears to leap across the frame in discrete jumps rather than fly smoothly. The information about where it was in between was never recorded, and no amount of cleverness afterwards can invent it back.

Sampling an audio or radio waveform is the identical act, applied to a wiggling voltage instead of a scene. The ADC photographs the wave's height at evenly spaced instants $t_n = n/f_s$. Grab enough snapshots per cycle and the numbers pin the waveform down uniquely — that is the whole promise of Nyquist–Shannon. Grab fewer than two per cycle and something worse than blur happens: the very same list of numbers also fits a slower wave just as well. The fast tone hasn't been degraded, it has been lost and silently replaced by a lower-frequency impostor — its alias. Nothing in the recorded data even hints that the original was the faster one. (The "Watch a tone alias past Nyquist" lab just below is the audio version of this: drag $f_s$ down and the dots peel away from the grey tone, leaving only the dashed impostor.)

The stroboscope — why a spun wheel can freeze or turn backwards

A stroboscope measures how fast something spins by flashing a light at a known rate and watching the apparent motion. It is sampling made visible. The grey spoke is the wheel's true position, turning continuously. The cyan spoke is what each flash freezes — it only updates at the sample instants, and the fading ghosts trace the motion your eye then reconstructs. When the flash rate $f_s$ is below twice the rotation rate, that reconstructed motion is wrong: the wheel can appear to crawl, stand still, or spin the other way — the same wagon-wheel effect you see on film.

Where the folding formula comes from

Between one sample and the next ($1/f_s$ seconds), a tone at $f$ advances $f/f_s$ cycles. Whole cycles are invisible — the wave is back at the same height when the sampler looks again — so only the leftover fraction of a cycle leaves a trace. In $f_\text{alias}=\lvert f - f_s\,\text{round}(f/f_s)\rvert$, the $\text{round}(f/f_s)$ counts the nearest whole number of cycles completed per sample interval, and subtracting $f_s$ times it removes them. The absolute value is there because a real-valued sampler can't tell a leftover of $+0.4$ cycles from $-0.4$ — spinning the other way produces the same heights. (Complex IQ sampling, Chapter 4, can tell the directions apart — that's why it gets the full $f_s$ of bandwidth.)

Rule of thumb

To represent a signal of bandwidth $B$ you need $f_s > 2B$. For a complex (IQ) stream — Chapter 4 — the usable bandwidth is the full $f_s$, not $f_s/2$, because positive and negative frequencies are distinct. That doubling is one of the quiet superpowers of complex sampling, and why SDRs almost always deliver IQ.

Practice problems

1. You want to capture a real-valued signal occupying a bandwidth of $B = 2.4$ MHz. By the Nyquist–Shannon rule $f_s > 2B$, what is the minimum real sample rate you must set?

Show solution
  1. Apply the rule. A real signal needs $f_s > 2B$.
  2. Compute. $2B = 2 \times 2.4 = 4.8$ MHz.
Answer: just above $4.8$ MS/s — anything lower folds the top of the band back down as aliasing.

2. A $1.8$ MHz tone is sampled at $f_s = 1$ MS/s — well above Nyquist. Using the folding formula $f_\text{alias} = \lvert f - f_s\,\text{round}(f/f_s)\rvert$, what apparent frequency does the ADC record?

Show solution
  1. Cycles per sample interval. $f/f_s = 1.8/1.0 = 1.8$, so $\text{round}(1.8) = 2$.
  2. Subtract the whole cycles. $\lvert 1.8\text{ MHz} - 1\text{ MHz}\times 2\rvert = \lvert -0.2\text{ MHz}\rvert$.
Answer: $200$ kHz — the unfiltered 1.8 MHz tone masquerades as a 200 kHz signal, which is why an anti-alias filter is mandatory.

3. Bandpass-sampling on purpose: a $5.5$ MHz signal is digitized at only $f_s = 4$ MS/s. Where in the first Nyquist zone does it land, by $f_\text{alias} = \lvert f - f_s\,\text{round}(f/f_s)\rvert$?

Show solution
  1. Nearest whole cycles. $f/f_s = 5.5/4 = 1.375$, so $\text{round} = 1$.
  2. Fold down. $\lvert 5.5\text{ MHz} - 4\text{ MHz}\times 1\rvert = 1.5$ MHz.
Answer: $1.5$ MHz — undersampling deliberately translates a high-frequency band down to baseband, the trick behind bandpass sampling.
Chapter 3 · From volts to numbers

Analogue to digital — the ADC, the DAC, and the cost of counting

How a microphone's smooth voltage becomes a list of integers, why a filter must sit in front of the converter, and how many bits it takes to do it cleanly

What was known

Sound is moving air. A microphone turns the rising and falling air pressure at its diaphragm into a rising and falling voltage — a smooth, continuous analogue signal. Between any two voltages lie infinitely many others, and between any two instants infinitely many more: an analogue signal carries an uncountable amount of detail.

The idea

A computer cannot store infinity. An analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) tames the signal twice: it samples in time (Chapter 2 — read the voltage only at instants $t_n=n/f_s$) and quantises in amplitude (round each reading to the nearest of a finite set of levels). What comes out is nothing but a sequence of integers.

The test

Rounding is never free: each sample is off by up to half a level, and that error behaves like added noise — quantisation noise. Use $N$ bits and you get $2^N$ levels; every extra bit halves the step and buys about 6 dB of dynamic range. A deliberate sprinkle of dither trades a harsh, signal-shaped error for benign hiss you can average away.

What it founds

Every sample your SDR hands you is an ADC reading, and every sound it plays back has passed through a digital-to-analogue converter (DAC). The anti-alias filter ahead of the ADC, the converter's bit depth, and the dither in its design are exactly what decide whether a capture is clean or riddled with phantom tones.

3.1 The microphone: pressure becomes a continuous voltage

Pluck a string and it shoves the air; the shove travels outward as a pressure wave; the wave nudges a microphone's diaphragm in and out. The microphone's only job is to report that motion as a voltage — more pressure, more voltage; less pressure, less voltage. The result is a continuous function of time: at every instant it has a definite value, and that value can be any real number in the microphone's range. There is no smallest step and no gap between instants — an analogue voltage is a point chosen from an infinite, unbroken continuum.

That smooth curve is the enemy of memory: to keep it exactly you would need infinitely many readings, each to infinite precision. The ADC's whole art is to discard almost all of that and retain just enough that the wave can still be rebuilt.

3.2 The ADC samples in time and quantises in amplitude

Two cuts turn the curve into numbers. Sampling (the vertical grid below) keeps the voltage only at instants $1/f_s$ apart — Chapter 2's story. Quantising (the horizontal grid) then snaps each reading to the nearest available level. With $N$ bits there are $2^N$ levels evenly spaced across the input range, separated by a step $\Delta = \text{range}/2^N$ — this even spacing is what "linear quantisation" means. The converter's output is just the index of the chosen level: an integer. Feed the list back through a DAC and each integer becomes a held voltage, redrawing the amber staircase; a gentle reconstruction filter then rounds the steps back into a smooth wave.

3.3 Why the anti-alias filter must sit before the ADC

Chapter 2 showed that any tone above $f_s/2$ folds down to a lower alias frequency. Here is the practical consequence: an analogue anti-alias filter — a low-pass filter that kills everything above $f_s/2$ — must be wired in between the microphone and the ADC. Unless you can already guarantee the signal contains nothing above the Nyquist limit, it is not optional. The reason it cannot wait until after digitising is worth spelling out.

∴ Why aliasing cannot be undone in software

Suppose a stray 9 kHz whistle reaches an ADC sampling at 10 kS/s, while your real signal lives at 1 kHz.

  1. Folding happens at the instant of sampling. By the folding formula the 9 kHz tone aliases to $\lvert 9-10\rvert = 1$ kHz. The moment each sample is taken, the whistle is recorded as if it were a 1 kHz tone.
  2. The two now share a frequency. Your genuine 1 kHz signal and the aliased whistle land on the same bin. Each stored sample is their sum — one number holding two contributions.
  3. Addition has no inverse. Given only "$a+b=7$" you cannot recover $a$ and $b$. No digital filter can separate two components that occupy the identical frequency, because a filter selects by frequency and these are indistinguishable. Filtering out 1 kHz would delete your real signal along with the alias.
  4. So the energy must be stopped while it is still distinguishable — while the whistle is still at 9 kHz, above Nyquist, in the analogue world. That is the only place it carries a label the filter can act on.
Conclusion: the anti-alias filter is the last chance to discard out-of-band energy before sampling fuses it with the signal forever. After the ADC it is too late — which is also why the mirror-image reconstruction filter after a DAC removes the spectral images sampling created.
3.4 How many bits do we need?

Bit depth sets the noise floor of the conversion, not its loudest sound. The quantisation error of a busy signal is roughly uniform over $\pm\Delta/2$, so its power is $\Delta^2/12$. Compare that to the power of the largest sine the converter can hold and you get a famous, almost suspiciously tidy rule: each bit is worth about 6 dB. The number of bits you need is therefore set by the dynamic range you must span — the gap between the faintest detail you care about and the loudest peak that must not clip.

The 6 dB-per-bit rule, derived

A full-scale sine has power $A^2/2$ with $A = 2^N\Delta/2$, so its power is $2^{2N}\Delta^2/8$. Quantisation-noise power is $\Delta^2/12$. The ratio is $\text{SQNR} = \dfrac{2^{2N}\Delta^2/8}{\Delta^2/12} = \tfrac{3}{2}\,2^{2N}$. In decibels, $10\log_{10}\!\big(\tfrac32 2^{2N}\big) = 20N\log_{10}2 + 10\log_{10}1.5 \approx \mathbf{6.02\,N + 1.76}$ dB. So 8-bit ≈ 50 dB (a basic RTL-SDR), 12-bit ≈ 74 dB (a better SDR), 16-bit ≈ 98 dB (CD audio), 24-bit ≈ 146 dB (studio). Pick $N$ so $6N$ comfortably exceeds the dynamic range your signal demands.

3.5 Dithering — adding noise to hear below the last bit

Quantisation error is not random when the signal is small and slow: a quiet tone that drifts across one or two levels gets rounded the same way cycle after cycle, so the error is locked to the signal. Locked error is the worst kind — it shows up in the spectrum as harmonic spurs, fake tones at multiples of the real frequency, that no averaging removes because they sit at fixed places. The counter-intuitive fix is to make things noisier: add a small, roughly one-level (1 LSB) random dither to the voltage before it is quantised.

Dither decorrelates the rounding from the signal. Now whether a given sample rounds up or down is decided partly by the dice, so on average the staircase tracks the true curve sub-step — the quantiser conveys information finer than its own step. In the spectrum the spurs collapse into a flat, benign noise floor, and because that floor is random you can average it down over many samples to reveal a tone that was quieter than a single bit.

Dither — trade spurs for hiss you can average away

A small, slow tone is quantised with only a few bits. Without dither the staircase steps in lockstep with the tone and its spectrum sprouts harmonic spikes. Turn dither on and a touch of noise is added before rounding: the spurs dissolve into a smooth floor. Crank the averaging up and that floor sinks, recovering the tone that was hiding below the least-significant bit. Watch the spectrum on the right.

Why noise can add information

It sounds like a paradox — corrupting the signal to improve it — but the gain is real and routine. Quantisation throws away the fractional part of each sample deterministically, so that fraction is lost identically every cycle. Dither converts the lost fraction into a probability: a sample sitting 0.3 of a step above a level rounds up about 30% of the time and down 70%, so the long-run average of many dithered samples equals the true 0.3 — the sub-step detail survives in the statistics even though no single sample can express it. Triangular-PDF dither of about 2 LSB peak is the standard choice in audio ADCs and is why 16-bit recordings can resolve detail well below their nominal step.

Chapter 4 · The heart of SDR

Complex numbers, or: why a radio sample has two parts

From the number line to $e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$ — and why your SDR delivers I and Q

Where this is going

Every sample from an SDR is a complex number — a pair $(I, Q)$. Not a quirk of the hardware: it is the most natural language for a signal that is fundamentally a rotation. This chapter builds complex numbers from scratch, the way the Great Scott Gadgets SDR series does — as the answer to a question about turning.

The question

On the ordinary number line, multiplication does two things. Scaling: $2\times 5$ moves you out along the line. And rotation: $-1 \times 5 = -5$ flips you to the other side — a half-turn through 180°. If multiplying by $-1$ is a half-turn, what number is a quarter-turn?

The answer

Call the quarter-turn $z$. Doing it twice is a half-turn, i.e. multiplying by $-1$: so $z \times z = -1$, meaning $z=\sqrt{-1}$. That number is $i$. A quarter-turn off the real line lands you on a brand-new axis — the imaginary axis — and complex numbers are simply points on the resulting 2-D plane.

What it founds

Multiplying by $i$ over and over walks you around a circle: $1\to i\to -1\to -i\to 1$. Repeated rotation is exactly what a radio carrier does, so $e^{i\omega t}$ — a number endlessly rotating — is the carrier. The two coordinates of that spinning point are the I and Q your SDR reports.

3.1 Multiplication can rotate, not just scale

Start on the number line — all the real numbers, rational and irrational, laid out left to right. Take the point at $5$. Multiply by $-1$: you land at $-5$. You can read that two ways. Either you scaled by $1$ and flipped sign, or — more usefully — you rotated the point a half-turn about the origin, swinging it 180° through the upper or lower half-plane to the far side. Multiplying by $-1$ is a 180° rotation.

∴ Solving for the quarter-turn

If a half-turn is "$\times(-1)$", a quarter-turn must be some number $z$ you apply twice to get a half-turn.

  1. Two quarter-turns make a half-turn: applying $z$ twice equals multiplying by $-1$. So for any point, $z\times(z\times \text{point}) = -\text{point}$, i.e. $z^2 = -1$.
  2. Solve: $z = \sqrt{-1}$. No real number squares to a negative, so $z$ cannot live on the number line — it points off it, at 90°.
  3. Name it $i$ (for "imaginary", though there is nothing imaginary about a quarter-turn). By construction $i^2=-1$.
  4. Then $1\cdot i$ sits at the point $(0,1)$, $2i$ at $(0,2)$, $3i$ at $(0,3)$ — the imaginary numbers are a vertical axis, perpendicular to the reals.
Conclusion: a complex number $a+bi$ is a point in a plane: $a$ along the real (horizontal) axis, $b$ along the imaginary (vertical) axis. Addition is just moving: $1+2i$ means start at $(1,0)$ and step up to $(1,2)$.

And the rotation keeps going. Start at $i$, the point $(0,1)$, and multiply by $i$ again: $i\times i = -1$, landing at $(-1,0)$. Once more: $\times i$ gives $-i$ at $(0,-1)$. Again: back to $1$. Multiplying by $i$ spins you 90° each time, forever — $1, i, -1, -i, 1, i, \dots$. A number whose powers cycle round a circle is the natural way to model anything periodic, which is to say: every wave.

NumPy speaks complex natively — 1j is $i$. Multiplying by 1j rotates by 90°:

3.2 Measuring the turn — radians, and why the base is $e$

To describe where on the circle you are, you need an angle. You could use degrees (a full turn = 360), but there is a more natural unit. A radian measures an angle by arc length on a circle of radius 1: walk a distance equal to the radius along the rim, and the angle you have swept is 1 radian. Since the full circumference is $2\pi r$, a whole turn is $2\pi r / r = 2\pi$ radians ≈ 6.28. Half a turn is $\pi$; a quarter-turn (our friend $i$) is $\pi/2$.

"Model rotation as raising some base to a power: $\text{base}^{\theta}$ steps you $\theta$ units around." Here is what that means. We already saw that multiplying by $i$ turns a point 90°, and multiplying again turns it another 90° — when you multiply, the angles add. That "adding by multiplying" is the defining signature of an exponent: $b^{a}\cdot b^{c}=b^{a+c}$. So if you want a single dial $\theta$ whose job is to rotate — where turning by $\theta_1$ and then by $\theta_2$ equals turning by $\theta_1+\theta_2$ — the natural object to reach for is $\text{base}^{\theta}$. Each unit you add to the exponent steps you one more fixed notch around the circle; the exponent literally counts how far you have turned. Rotation is exponentiation with the angle living in the exponent.

Which base? Any base will rotate, but with most of them the exponent and the actual swept angle differ by a stretch factor of $\ln(\text{base})$ — an annoying conversion bolted onto every calculation. The base that makes it vanish is $e\approx2.718$, defined as the one function whose slope equals its own height everywhere: the rate of change of $e^{x}$ is $e^{x}$ itself. Choosing $e$ sets that stretch factor to exactly 1, so the exponent reads straight off as radians — $e^{i\theta}$ lands precisely $\theta$ radians around the unit circle, with no conversion in between. That is the whole reason $e$, and not 2 or 10, sits under every carrier in this document.

"Feed it an imaginary exponent and that 'growth' turns into pure rotation." The trick is to watch the velocity — which way the point is heading at each instant. For a real exponent, $\frac{d}{dt}e^{t}=e^{t}$: the velocity points the same direction as the position itself, so the point flees straight out along the line it is already on — that outward-feeding-on-itself motion is exponential growth. Now slip an $i$ into the exponent: $\frac{d}{d\theta}e^{i\theta}=i\,e^{i\theta}$. The velocity is still proportional to the position, but multiplied by $i$ — that is, the position vector turned 90°. A velocity locked forever perpendicular to the radius can never lengthen it: the point gets no nearer and no farther from the origin, it can only swing around. Same engine, redirected a quarter-turn — real exponents grow outward, imaginary exponents spin in place. $e^{i\theta}$ doesn't grow; it circles, at unit speed, sweeping exactly $\theta$ radians.

The contrast in panels 3 & 4 is the whole idea: a real exponent pushes the point along its radius (growth); the extra $i$ turns that push 90° across the radius (rotation).

∴ Euler's formula and Euler's identity

Two equivalent ways to move in a circle — the relation Euler discovered.

  1. Drop a line from the point $e^{i\theta}$ to the real axis: a right triangle with hypotenuse 1 (the radius). Its horizontal leg is $\cos\theta$, its vertical leg is $\sin\theta$ (SOH-CAH-TOA on a unit hypotenuse — the same circle definition Chapter 0 built sine from).
  2. The horizontal position is the real part, the vertical is the imaginary part. So the point is $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ — Euler's formula. This is the bridge between rotation and the two sinusoids.
  3. Put in a half-turn, $\theta=\pi$: $\cos\pi=-1$, $\sin\pi=0$, giving $e^{i\pi} = -1$, i.e. $e^{i\pi}+1=0$ — Euler's identity, tying $e$, $i$, $\pi$, $1$ and $0$ together. A full turn $\theta=2\pi$ returns $e^{i\,2\pi}=1$.
Intuition (BetterExplained): $1\cdot e^{i\pi}$ reads as "start at 1, and instead of growing, rotate for $\pi$ units of time" — $\pi$ radians, a half-circle — arriving at $-1$. Imaginary exponents rotate; real exponents grow.

So a steadily rotating complex number, $e^{i\,2\pi f t}$, traces the unit circle $f$ times a second. Whenever you see $e^{i\omega t}$, picture a point spinning — or, unrolled along a time axis, a three-dimensional helix whose two shadows are a cosine (the I axis) and a sine (the Q axis). That helix is the cleanest possible model of a carrier wave.

A complex carrier in one line — its real part is a cosine, its imaginary part a sine, 90° apart:

f  = 50_000
t  = np.arange(2000) / fs

Q = z.imag        # = sin(2πft)   the quadrature component, a quarter-cycle behind

# the radio stores exactly these two arrays. |z| is the amplitude envelope,
# np.angle(z) is the instantaneous phase. Change in phase per sample = frequency:
inst_freq = np.angle(z[1:] * np.conj(z[:-1]))   # <- the FM demod in decode_fsk.py

The spinning phasor and its two shadows

A point rotates around the unit circle at the frequency you set. Its horizontal shadow traces the cosine (I) in amber; its vertical shadow traces the sine (Q) in green. This is $e^{i\omega t}$ unrolled in time — the single object behind both sinusoids. Press play and watch one rotation become two waves a quarter-cycle apart.

The payoff — I/Q, in-phase and quadrature

This is why an SDR sample is complex. The hardware multiplies the incoming RF by both a cosine and a sine of the tuner frequency (90° apart — "in quadrature") and low-pass filters each, giving two streams: I (in-phase, the real axis) and Q (quadrature, the imaginary axis). Together they are one complex number per instant. Because the rotation direction is preserved, $+f$ and $-f$ are distinguishable — so complex sampling captures the full bandwidth $f_s$ and can tell a signal above the tuned frequency from one below it. The point on the unit circle is sometimes called the magnitude, modulus, amplitude, or absolute value; its two coordinates are the I and Q the rest of this companion decodes.

Chapter 5 · The trick the Fourier transform plays

From a spinning point to the Fourier coefficient

Why "how much of frequency f is in this signal" is literally an average of the signal against a spinning unit vector — the idea the FFT automates

What was known

Chapter 4 built one spinning phasor, $e^{i\omega t}$ — a point endlessly circling the unit circle, its two shadows the cosine ($I$) and sine ($Q$). A pure tone is such a point. The question this chapter answers: given a recorded signal, how do you discover which spinning points it was made of, and how fast each was turning?

The idea

Film a point going round a circle of radius 1, three turns a second. Take 16 photos a second and lay them in a row. The point's height across the frames traces a sine; the points themselves, joined in order, draw a line that twists — a helix seen flat-on.

The test

Now spin every frame backwards by some trial rate before you look. At one special rate — exactly the point's own — the twist unwinds into a straight line: every frame shows the point at the same angle. Asking "do the frames line up, or cancel out?" at every possible rate is, in one sentence, the Fourier transform.

What it founds

Lining the frames up and averaging them is exactly multiplying the signal by $e^{-i2\pi f t}$ and summing. The length of that average is how much of frequency $f$ is present; its angle is the phase. That single complex number is the Fourier coefficient — and Chapter 6's FFT is just this average computed at every frequency at once.

5.3 Rotate the frames until the line is straight

Here is the move that unlocks everything. Before looking at frame $n$, rotate it backwards by a trial rate $g$ — turn it by $-2\pi g\,n/f_s$. If your trial rate is too slow or too fast, the line still twists, just less or in the other direction. But hit the trial rate exactly equal to the point's own and every frame's backward-spin cancels its forward-spin: all the points snap to the same angle and the joining line goes dead straight. The points stop scattering around the circle and pile up in one place — so their average (the resultant arrow) suddenly grows long. Off the right rate, the de-rotated points fan out evenly around the circle and average to nearly nothing.

Why this is a measurement, not a trick. Frame $n$ caught the point at angle $2\pi f\,n/f_s$. De-rotating that frame by trial rate $g$ subtracts $2\pi g\,n/f_s$, leaving a residual angle of $2\pi(f-g)\,n/f_s$. As long as $g\neq f$ that residual keeps growing with the frame number $n$ — the points step around the circle and the joining line corkscrews. The moment $g=f$ the residual is zero for every single frame at once: all 16 points snap to the same angle, the line goes dead straight, and their average — the resultant — reaches its full length of 1. So the trial rate that straightens the line is, by construction, the point's true turning rate. You have measured the frequency without ever counting a single turn — and that is the whole of the Fourier transform in one move. 5.4 Compound motion — two circles at once

Real signals are not one tidy circle. Picture a small circle whose centre rides around a big one — a planet with a moon, or a big slow turn carrying a quick little wobble. Mathematically that is just the sum of two spinning points: $z_n = a_1 e^{i2\pi f_1 n/f_s} + a_2 e^{i2\pi f_2 n/f_s}$. The beauty of the de-rotation test is that it is selective. Spin the frames backwards at $f_1$ and the first point freezes and piles up while the second keeps whirling and averages to zero — so the resultant reports $a_1$ alone. Spin at $f_2$ and you read off $a_2$. Each circular motion answers only to its own trial rate, which is why sweeping $g$ across all rates (the spectrum plot above) pulls a compound motion apart into its separate turns.

This is the doorway to the Fourier transform. Run the idea backwards: if any signal can be built by stacking spinning circles of the right rates and sizes — and it can — then analysing a signal is nothing more than discovering which circles it was built from. The de-rotation test above is exactly that discovery, done one rate at a time: ask "is there a circle turning at $g$?" by spinning every frame backwards at $g$ and measuring how much piles up. Do it for a single $g$ and you have computed one Fourier coefficient; sweep $g$ across all rates and the collected answers — an amplitude and a phase at each — are the signal's full spectrum. That sweep is the Fourier transform. 5.5 Sines, cosines, and the Fourier coefficient

Two languages, one object. A spinning point and a pair of waves are the same thing wearing different clothes — that is exactly what Euler's formula says.

∴ From "line up the frames" to $X(f)=\frac1N\sum x_n e^{-i2\pi f n/f_s}$

Take the de-rotation test and write it down symbol by symbol.

  1. A frame is a complex number. Frame $n$ catches the point at angle $\theta_n = 2\pi f n/f_s$, so $z_n = e^{i\theta_n} = \cos\theta_n + i\sin\theta_n$. Its horizontal shadow traces a cosine wave, its vertical shadow a sine — "a sum of cosines and sines" and "a sum of spinning points" are the very same description, bridged by Euler.
  2. De-rotating is multiplying by a backward spin. Turning frame $n$ back by trial rate $g$ multiplies it by $e^{-i2\pi g n/f_s}$. The de-rotated frame is $z_n\,e^{-i2\pi g n/f_s}$.
  3. Averaging is the resultant. Add the de-rotated frames and divide by $N$: $X(g)=\frac1N\sum_{n}z_n\,e^{-i2\pi g n/f_s}$. When $g$ equals a turning rate in the signal, every term points the same way and they add; otherwise the unit vectors are spread evenly and cancel. So $\lvert X(g)\rvert$ is how much of frequency $g$ is present and $\arg X(g)$ is its phase.
  4. Nothing changes for a real signal. Replace the ideal $z_n$ with your actual samples $x_n$ (real voltages, or complex IQ) and the formula stands: $X(g)=\frac1N\sum_n x_n\,e^{-i2\pi g n/f_s}$. That is the Fourier coefficient at frequency $g$.
Conclusion: the Fourier transform is the de-rotation test run at every frequency. Evaluate $X$ only at the special frequencies $g = k\,f_s/N$ (a whole number of turns per window, so the cancellation is exact) and you get the discrete Fourier transform; compute all $N$ of them with the divide-and-conquer shortcut and you have the FFT — the subject of Chapter 6.

Why off-frequency points cancel

The $N$ de-rotated unit vectors at trial rate $g$ sit at angles $2\pi(f-g)n/f_s$ for $n=0,1,\dots,N-1$ — equally spaced steps around the circle. Equally spaced points on a circle sum to zero (every vector has an opposite), so the average collapses to nearly nothing unless the step is zero, i.e. $g=f$. That all-or-nothing behaviour is what makes the average a clean frequency detector rather than a smear, and it is the same orthogonality that lets OFDM (Chapter 10) stack carriers without crosstalk.

Chapter 6 · From time to frequency

The Fourier transform — every signal is a sum of spinning phasors

np.fft · turning a wall of samples into a spectrum, and reading a 847.5 kHz line straight off it

What was known

Chapter 4 built one rotating phasor $e^{i\omega t}$. Fourier's claim is that any signal — however jagged — is a sum of such phasors at different frequencies, each with its own amplitude and starting phase. The Fourier transform is the machine that, given the samples, hands back that recipe: how much of each frequency is present.

The idea

To measure "how much 50 kHz is in this signal", multiply the signal by a 50 kHz phasor and sum. If the signal contains that frequency, the products line up and the sum is large; if not, they cancel to nearly zero. Do this for every frequency bin and you get the spectrum. The FFT is just a fast algorithm for that sum — $O(N\log N)$ instead of $O(N^2)$.

The test

Sum three tones, take the FFT, and three sharp spikes appear at exactly those frequencies — the transform un-mixes what addition mixed. Bin spacing is $f_s/N$: more samples buys finer frequency resolution. With complex IQ input, the spectrum is two-sided, so $+f$ and $-f$ are separate spikes.

What it founds

decode_nfc.py opens each card response with a power spectrum (scipy.signal.welch) and the 847.5 kHz subcarrier line appears as a bright peak — direct confirmation the card is load-modulating at $f_c/16$, before a single bit is decoded. The FFT is the universal "what's in this capture?" tool.

The whole transform is three lines — and the PSD trick decode_nfc.py uses to find the subcarrier:

X     = np.fft.fft(x)                 # complex spectrum, one bin per frequency
freqs = np.fft.fftfreq(len(x), 1/fs)  # the Hz value of each bin
mag   = np.abs(X)                     # amplitude at each frequency

# bin spacing (frequency resolution) = fs / N
# peak frequency:
f_peak = freqs[np.argmax(mag[:len(x)//2])]

# decode_nfc.py instead uses Welch's method (averaged FFTs = smoother PSD):
from scipy.signal import welch
f_psd, p_psd = welch(envelope, fs=fs, nperseg=8192)   # 847.5 kHz line pops out

Why a sharp tone makes a sharp spike

Multiplying the signal by a test phasor $e^{-i2\pi f_k t}$ and summing is a correlation: it asks "how aligned is the signal with this frequency?" A matching tone stays perfectly in step over the whole window, so every term adds — a tall spike. A non-matching frequency drifts in and out of phase and its terms cancel. The same correlation logic reappears in decode_fsk.py, which cross-correlates the demodulated bits against a known access code to find where a packet starts.

Why the bins are fs/N apart — and what Welch trades away

$N$ samples span $N/f_s$ seconds. The DFT's test phasors are precisely the frequencies that complete a whole number of cycles in that window — 0, 1, 2, … — because only those line up with themselves end to end. One cycle per window is $f_s/N$ Hz, so the bins step by $f_s/N$: capture longer and the frequency comb gets finer. Welch's method (the scipy.signal.welch call above) deliberately goes the other way — it chops the capture into shorter pieces, FFTs each, and averages the results. The noise floor smooths out, making a real peak much easier to trust, at the price of coarser bins: resolution traded for confidence. That is why decode_nfc.py uses it just to ask "is the 847.5 kHz line there at all?"

Chapter 7 · Putting data on a wave

Modulation — making a carrier carry bits

AM · FM · ASK/OOK · FSK · PSK — the five ways to vary a sine, and the demod that reverses each

What was known

A bare carrier $\cos(2\pi f_c t)$ carries no information — it is the same forever. To send data you must vary one of its three knobs over time: amplitude, frequency, or phase. Each choice is a modulation scheme, and each has a matching demodulator that reads the knob back out.

The three knobs

Amplitude → AM / ASK / OOK: make the carrier louder and softer. Frequency → FM / FSK: shift the pitch between two tones for 0 and 1. Phase → PM / PSK: jump the phase by 180° to flip a bit. The digital versions (ASK, FSK, PSK) just use a few discrete levels of the analog knob.

The test

Switch schemes in the lab and watch the same bit pattern reshape the carrier. The demod is the inverse: AM → take the envelope $|z|$; FSK → take the instantaneous frequency $\angle(z_n\,\overline{z_{n-1}})$; PSK → track the phase. The envelope and inst-freq operators are literally what the decoders call.

What it founds

The protocols ahead are each a modulation choice: NFC is ASK/OOK (amplitude — a switched subcarrier), POCSAG paging is plain 2-FSK (frequency, hard-switched), Bluetooth LE is GFSK (frequency, smoothed), Wi-Fi is QAM on many subcarriers (amplitude+phase at once). Learn the five knobs and every real signal is a combination of them.

Each scheme is one line of NumPy on the carrier — and its demod is one line back. From decode_fsk.py:

t  = np.arange(n) / fs
car = np.exp(1j * 2*np.pi*fc * t)        # complex carrier

# --- modulate (msg is the 0/1 bit waveform, sampled at sps samples/bit) ---
am   = (1 + 0.8*msg) * car                       # AM / ASK: vary amplitude
ook  = msg * car                                 # OOK: carrier on for 1, off for 0
fsk  = np.exp(1j*2*np.pi*(fc + dev*(2*msg-1))*t) # 2-FSK: +dev for 1, −dev for 0
bpsk = np.exp(1j*(2*np.pi*fc*t + np.pi*msg))     # BPSK: 180° phase flip per bit

# --- demodulate ---
env  = np.abs(z)                                 # AM/OOK: amplitude envelope
freq = np.angle(z[1:] * np.conj(z[:-1]))         # FSK: instantaneous frequency
# (decode_fsk.py boxcar-averages `freq` over each symbol, then slices the sign)

The instantaneous-frequency one-liner, unpacked

The expression $\angle(z_n\,\overline{z_{n-1}})$ looks cryptic but is three small moves. Each complex sample is a point on the circle, $z_n = e^{i\phi_n}$. Conjugating flips its spin: $\overline{z_{n-1}} = e^{-i\phi_{n-1}}$. Multiply them and the angles subtract: $z_n\overline{z_{n-1}} = e^{i(\phi_n - \phi_{n-1})}$. So np.angle of that product is simply $\Delta\phi$ — how much phase advanced in one sample. Frequency is the rate of phase change (Chapter 0: rotation rate), so in Hz it is $\Delta\phi \cdot f_s/2\pi$. For 2-FSK the decoders never even convert: a 1-bit spins the phasor one way ($\Delta\phi>0$), a 0-bit the other, so the sign alone is the bit.

Chapter 8 · POCSAG paging at 439.98 MHz

Pagers — the simplest digital protocol still on the air

2-FSK · 1200 baud · ±4.5 kHz deviation — captured on a HackRF, decoded in Universal Radio Hacker (URH)

What a pager is

A pager is a pocket-sized, one-way radio receiver. A base station blankets an area with a single high-power transmission; every pager in range hears it, but each only reacts when it sees its own address — a unique number burned in at the factory called the RIC (Receiver Identity Code). Hit, it beeps and shows a short numeric or text message. No uplink, no handshake, no association — which is exactly why hospitals, fire services and industrial sites still run them: simplex broadcast penetrates buildings, covers huge areas from one tower, and a coin cell lasts weeks.

The modulation

The bits ride the carrier as plain 2-FSK: a 1 shifts the carrier up by the deviation, a 0 shifts it down. POCSAG uses ±4.5 kHz deviation at 512, 1200 or 2400 baud — here 1200. It is the bare frequency knob of Chapter 7, with none of Bluetooth's Gaussian smoothing, so the bits are hard NRZ steps you can slice one sample at a time.

The protocol — POCSAG

POCSAG (Post Office Code Standardisation Advisory Group; CCIR Radiopaging Code No. 1) wraps those bits in a rigid frame: a long preamble of alternating 1010… to lock the receiver's bit clock, then repeating batches, each a 32-bit sync codeword followed by 8 frames of two 32-bit codewords. Every codeword is BCH(31,21) error-protected with an even-parity tail.

The decode

Capture at 439.98 MHz, 1 MS/s on a HackRF; demodulate FSK by the sign of the instantaneous frequency (Chapter 4's $\angle(z_n\overline{z_{n-1}})$); find the 1010 preamble and the sync word 0x7CD215D8; slice 32-bit codewords and read the address and message. URH does every step interactively — and the captures below show it on real "Hello" / "aaaaa" pages.

8.1 How a pager actually worked

The whole system is built around saving the pager's battery. A pager cannot transmit, so it can never say "I'm here" — the network simply broadcasts to everyone and trusts each unit to recognise itself. To avoid keeping the receiver awake through every message in the city, POCSAG slices each batch into 8 frames and assigns every pager to one of them by its address. The pager wakes its receiver only during its own frame, checks for its RIC, and sleeps through the other seven. That is why the 21-bit RIC is split: 18 bits live in the address codeword, and the remaining 3 bits are implied by which frame the codeword arrived in (frame 0–7 = 3 bits). The seven-digit number on the back of the pager is those 21 bits in decimal.

Why one-way paging refuses to die

Because there is no return path, a paging transmitter can run high power from a single tall site and still reach a coin-cell receiver deep inside a building — no negotiation, no congestion collapse when thousands of devices answer at once. The same one-to-many simplicity that makes paging feel antique is what keeps it as the fallback when cellular fails: emergency and hospital alerting still leans on it precisely because it has no moving parts to break under load.

8.2 The modulation — 2-FSK, sliced by the sign of the frequency

There is no constellation and no subcarrier here. The transmitter holds the carrier at $f_c + 4.5$ kHz for a 1 and $f_c - 4.5$ kHz for a 0, switching 1200 times a second. To read it back you do not even need to convert to Hz: by Chapter 4, the instantaneous frequency is $\angle(z_n\,\overline{z_{n-1}})$, and for two-level FSK a 1-bit makes that phase step positive and a 0-bit makes it negative — the sign alone is the bit. That is the entire FSK demodulator, the same one decode_fsk.py runs for Bluetooth.

Two tones, one knob

POCSAG's deviation (±4.5 kHz) is small next to its bit rate, so the two tones sit close together — which is why the spectrogram below looks like a single fuzzy band that thickens and thins rather than two cleanly separated lines. The decoder doesn't care: it watches the direction the phasor turns, and a tiny deviation still flips the sign reliably as long as the SNR holds.

The whole FSK demod is the Chapter 4 operator plus a slice — mirroring pocsag_generator.py's inverse:

8.3 The POCSAG frame — preamble, batches, codewords

Once the bits are sliced, structure is everything. POCSAG nests three levels: the whole transmission opens with a long preamble, then repeats batches; each batch is one sync codeword plus 8 frames; each frame is two 32-bit codewords. The preamble is at least 576 bits of alternating 1010… (it shows up as 0xAAAAAAAA repeated) — its only job is to give the receiver's clock-recovery a steady stream of edges to lock onto before any data arrives. Then the fixed sync word 0x7CD215D8 marks the start of every batch, so the decoder always knows where the 32-bit grid begins.

8.4 Inside a codeword — address vs message

Every 32-bit codeword announces its type with its first bit: 0 for an address codeword, 1 for a message codeword. An address codeword carries 18 bits of the RIC, 2 function bits (which of four message types / which "beep"), then a 10-bit BCH check and a final even-parity bit. A message codeword spends 20 bits on payload, then the same 10-bit BCH check and parity. The BCH(31,21) code can correct up to two bit errors per codeword and detect more — essential when a page might be received at the fringe of coverage with no way to ask for a retransmit.

8.5 Numeric and alphanumeric messages

Twenty message bits per codeword get packed two ways. Numeric paging uses a 4-bit character set (digits, a few symbols, space), so each codeword holds exactly 5 characters — but the bits of each character are sent least-significant-first, a reversal you must undo before reading. Alphanumeric paging uses 7-bit ASCII, so characters don't divide evenly into 20 bits: they straddle codeword boundaries, streaming bit-after-bit across the whole message and padding the final codeword with end-of-text or null. POCSAG caps a standard alphanumeric message at 40 characters (with provisions for up to 80).

Why the preamble is 1010… — and how the sync word breaks it

Alternating 1010… is the densest possible stream of transitions, which is exactly what a receiver's bit-clock recovery needs to lock its timing (the same edge-hunger that makes Manchester coding and BLE whitening worthwhile in Chapters 9–10). Chapter 9 · ISO 14443-A at 13.56 MHz

NFC — a card whispering in a subcarrier

Manchester · load modulation · the 847.5 kHz subcarrier — straight from nfc_modulation_demo.py

The setup

An NFC reader (PCD) holds an unmodulated 13.56 MHz carrier in the air. A passive card (PICC) has no battery — it harvests power from that field by near-field magnetic coupling, like the secondary of a loose air-core transformer. To answer, it cannot transmit its own carrier; it must modulate the reader's.

The card's voice

The card answers by switching a load resistor across its coil at $f_c/16 = $ 847.5 kHz — a subcarrier. To the reader this looks like a tiny AM ripple (a few percent) on its own carrier: a pair of sidebands at $13.56\,\text{MHz}\pm847.5\,\text{kHz}$. That ripple is the entire payload.

Encoding the bits

The card OOK-keys that subcarrier with a Manchester code: a 1 is subcarrier-on then off, a 0 is off then on — every bit has a guaranteed mid-edge and is DC-balanced. One bit (etu) is $f_c/128 = $ 9.44 µs = 8 subcarrier cycles. Each byte ships 8 data bits LSB-first plus one odd-parity bit.

The decode

decode_nfc.py reverses it exactly: bandpass the envelope around 847.5 kHz, AM-detect with a Hilbert transform, slice each half-etu, and recover bits → bytes → the card's UID. The other direction (reader→card) is 100% OOK using Modified Miller pauses, handled in nfc_modulation_demo.py.

Manchester encoding and the gated subcarrier — the core of nfc_modulation_demo.py:

CARRIER_HZ    = 13.56e6
SUBCARRIER_HZ = CARRIER_HZ / 16      # 847.5 kHz
ETU_S         = 128 / CARRIER_HZ     # 9.44 µs per bit (8 subcarrier cycles)

def byte_to_bits_with_parity(byte):
    bits = [(byte >> i) & 1 for i in range(8)]   # 8 data bits, LSB-first
    bits.append(1 - (sum(bits) & 1))             # + 1 odd-parity bit
    return bits

def manchester_halfbits(bits):
    out = []
    for b in bits:
        out.extend([1, 0] if b else [0, 1])      # '1' = on-off,  '0' = off-on
    return out

# the card gates a pure 847.5 kHz sine with that half-bit pattern, then
# load-modulates the 13.56 MHz carrier:  rf = carrier * (1 + m * mod_subcarrier)

"Odd parity" and "DC-balanced", unpacked

Odd parity: the ninth bit is chosen so that the nine bits together contain an odd number of 1s. If any single bit flips in transit, the count turns even and the receiver knows the byte is corrupt — a one-bit error detector that costs one bit. (That is the 1 - (sum(bits) & 1) in the code above.) DC-balanced: in Manchester coding every bit spends exactly half its time "on" and half "off", so the waveform's long-run average never drifts. That matters because the reader recovers the data from an AM envelope with a decision threshold — a drifting average would drag the threshold around — and the guaranteed mid-bit edge in every single bit gives the decoder a clock to lock onto, even through a long run of identical bits.

Show solution
  1. Bit duration. $128/f_c = 128/13{,}560{,}000 \approx 9.44\times10^{-6}$ s $= 9.44$ µs.
  2. Bit rate. $f_c/128 = 13{,}560{,}000/128 \approx 105{,}937$ bit/s.
Answer: $9.44$ µs per bit, $\approx 106$ kbps — the standard ISO 14443-A baseline data rate.

3. How many full subcarrier cycles fit inside one etu? Combine $f_\text{sub} = f_c/16$ and the bit duration $T_\text{etu} = 128/f_c$ as $f_\text{sub} \times T_\text{etu}$.

Show solution
  1. Multiply. $f_\text{sub} \times T_\text{etu} = \dfrac{f_c}{16}\cdot\dfrac{128}{f_c}$.
  2. The carrier cancels. $= 128/16 = 8$.
Answer: exactly $8$ subcarrier cycles per bit — which is why each Manchester half-bit holds 4 clean cycles to gate on or off.
Chapter 10 · Bluetooth LE at 2.4 GHz

Bluetooth — GFSK, the polite cousin of FSK

1 Msym/s · 40 channels · Gaussian-smoothed frequency shift — the family decode_remote.py targets

What was known

Bluetooth Low Energy lives in the 2.4 GHz ISM band and sends 1 million symbols per second using GFSK — frequency-shift keying, like Chapter 7's FSK, but with the bit edges Gaussian-smoothed first. The smoothing rounds the abrupt frequency jumps so the signal occupies less bandwidth and doesn't splatter into neighbouring channels.

The channels

BLE divides the band into 40 channels 2 MHz apart, $f = 2402 + 2k$ MHz. Three of them — channels 37, 38, 39 at 2402, 2426, 2480 MHz — are the advertising channels, deliberately spread across the band so a crowded 2.4 GHz environment can't block all three. Devices hop the other 37 for data once connected.

The trade-off

The Gaussian filter is described by BT, its bandwidth-time product (BLE uses 0.5). Lower BT = more smoothing = narrower spectrum but more inter-symbol interference (each bit smears into its neighbours). That ISI is exactly why decode_fsk.py notes a GFSK decoder needs a one-symbol differential detector, while plain FSK can be sliced per sample.

The decode

decode_remote.py scans the 2.4 GHz ISM band, finds the narrowband carrier of a toy remote or BLE device, estimates its GFSK symbol rate, and demodulates with the same instantaneous-frequency operator. The same machinery reads BLE advertisements (the ble_ch37.iq capture) — find the access address, dewhiten, and the packet falls out.

GFSK is FSK with a Gaussian pre-filter on the bits — and the demod is the same inst-freq from Chapter 4:

fs       = 2e6
sym_rate = 1_000_000            # BLE: 1 Msym/s
sps      = int(fs / sym_rate)  # samples per symbol
dev      = 250e3               # ~250 kHz frequency deviation (BLE index ~0.5)

# Gaussian pulse-shape the NRZ bits before FM modulating (this is the 'G' in GFSK)
from scipy.signal import gaussian
shape = gaussian(sps, std=sps*0.5/2.3548)      # BT = 0.5
shaped = np.convolve(np.repeat(2*bits-1, sps), shape/shape.sum(), 'same')
phase  = np.cumsum(2*np.pi*dev*shaped/fs)
tx     = np.exp(1j*phase)

# receive: instantaneous frequency, exactly as decode_remote.py / decode_fsk.py do
freq = np.angle(rx[1:] * np.conj(rx[:-1]))     # > 0 -> bit 1, < 0 -> bit 0

# BLE channel center frequencies:
chan = lambda k: 2402e6 + 2e6*k                # k = 0..39;  37/38/39 = advertising

FSK vs GFSK — what the Gaussian filter buys

The same bit pattern, FM-modulated two ways: hard FSK (grey, sharp frequency steps) and GFSK (amber, Gaussian-smoothed). Lower the BT product and watch the GFSK curve round off harder — narrower spectrum, but the levels start blurring together (the ISI that complicates the decoder). The plot is instantaneous frequency, the very thing the receiver slices.

The "G" is Chapter 0's bell curve — used as a filter, not as noise

Here the Gaussian isn't randomness: it is a set of weights. The np.convolve in the code slides a bell-shaped window along the square bit waveform, replacing each point with a bell-weighted average of its neighbours — which turns every cliff-edge frequency jump into a smooth S-curve. Sharp edges are what cost bandwidth (recall Chapter 1's square wave, which needs harmonics far above its base frequency), so smoothing the edges directly narrows the transmitted spectrum. BT is the filter's bandwidth multiplied by the bit duration: BT = 0.5 means the filter passes only about half the bit rate, so each bit's transition bleeds into its neighbours — the inter-symbol interference the lab shows. And the odd constant 2.3548 in the code is $2\sqrt{2\ln 2}$: it converts a width quoted as "full width at half maximum" into the σ that scipy.signal.gaussian expects.

And "dewhiten"? BLE XORs every packet with a known pseudo-random bit pattern before transmission ("whitening") so the air never carries long runs of identical bits — runs that would put all the energy in one spot and starve the receiver's timing recovery of edges. The receiver XORs with the same pattern to undo it: XOR twice and the data is back.

Chapter 11 · Wi-Fi — OFDM & QAM

Wi-Fi — hundreds of tiny carriers, each a constellation

The IFFT as a transmitter · QAM symbols · why your router sends 64 sinusoids at once

The problem

NFC and BLE send one symbol stream on one carrier. Wi-Fi needs to push tens of megabits through a channel full of echoes (multipath), where a single fast stream smears unrecoverably. The fix — OFDM, orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing — splits the data across many slow subcarriers running in parallel, each so slow that echoes barely bother it.

The trick

A 20 MHz Wi-Fi channel is divided into 64 subcarriers 312.5 kHz apart. Each carries a few bits as a QAM symbol — a point in the complex plane whose amplitude and phase encode the bits. The genius: a transmitter builds all 64 at once with a single inverse FFT. The IFFT is the modulator; the receiver's FFT is the demodulator. Chapter 6, run backwards.

The constellation

More points per subcarrier = more bits per symbol, but points sit closer together and noise confuses them sooner. BPSK carries 1 bit/subcarrier, QPSK 2, 16-QAM 4, 64-QAM 6. A clean link with high SNR uses dense 64-QAM; a weak one falls back to rugged BPSK. The received cloud of dots — the constellation diagram — is the first thing you plot to judge link quality.

The thread

It is all the same toolkit: complex IQ (Chapter 4) for the constellation points, the FFT (Chapter 6) for the (de)modulator, and phase/amplitude keying (Chapter 7) raised to dozens of carriers at once. Decoding real Wi-Fi adds synchronisation and equalisation, but the heart is an FFT of the IQ you now know how to capture.

An OFDM symbol is an IFFT of QAM points — the transmitter in a few lines:

N = 64                                   # subcarriers (20 MHz Wi-Fi)
fs = 20e6
df = fs / N                              # 312.5 kHz subcarrier spacing

# map bits to 16-QAM points (amplitude+phase = a complex number per subcarrier)
levels = np.array([-3,-1,1,3])
symbols = (np.random.choice(levels, N) + 1j*np.random.choice(levels, N)) / np.sqrt(10)

# the IFFT turns 64 frequency-domain QAM points into one time-domain OFDM symbol
ofdm_time = np.fft.ifft(symbols)
ofdm_time = np.concatenate([ofdm_time[-16:], ofdm_time])   # add cyclic prefix

# receiver: strip prefix, FFT back to the constellation, slice each point
rx_syms = np.fft.fft(ofdm_time[16:])     # -> recovers the QAM points

Two details in the code, unpacked

The √10: 16-QAM uses the levels $\pm1, \pm3$ on each axis, so the average symbol power is $E[I^2]+E[Q^2] = 5+5 = 10$. Dividing every point by $\sqrt{10}$ normalizes the constellation to average power 1, so BPSK, QPSK and 64-QAM can be compared at the same transmit power and a given noise level means the same thing for each. (Each order has its own factor: $\sqrt 2$ for QPSK, $\sqrt{42}$ for 64-QAM.) The cyclic prefix: the last 16 samples of each symbol are copied onto its front. Multipath echoes of the previous symbol then land in that sacrificial pad instead of the data — and because the pad is a copy of the tail, every subcarrier still looks perfectly periodic to the receiver's FFT even when the whole symbol arrives slightly delayed.

Why "orthogonal"

The subcarriers are spaced at exactly $f_s/N$ so that over one symbol each completes a whole number of cycles and integrates to zero against every other — they are orthogonal, so despite overlapping spectra they don't interfere, and the FFT separates them perfectly. That is the same correlation-cancels-unless-aligned property that makes the Fourier transform work in Chapter 6. OFDM is, quite literally, communicating in FFT bins.
